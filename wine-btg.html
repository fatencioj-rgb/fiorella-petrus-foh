The Producer

Palmer & Co is a house built on the idea of patience. While many Champagne producers rush their wines to market, Palmer ages its non-vintage Brut for four years on the lees in standard bottles and six years in Magnum, three times what the appellation requires. That commitment to time defines everything about the house.

What makes La Réserve particularly interesting is its perpetual reserve system. The reserve wines used in each blend are composed of previous blends of La Réserve itself, meaning each bottle carries a thread of DNA from every vintage that came before it. It is a wine that literally contains its own history. Half the grapes come from Grand Cru and Premier Cru vineyards, and up to 35% of the final blend is reserve wine, giving a depth and maturity that belies its accessible positioning.

The Magnum format is not just a larger bottle here. The slower, more graceful ageing that occurs in Magnum means the wine develops differently: more complexity, finer bubbles, greater harmony.

Viticulture

Grapes are sourced from Grand Cru and Premier Cru vineyards across the Montagne de Reims and the Côte des Blancs. The Chardonnay-dominant blend draws its precision and freshness from the chalk soils of these classified villages, while Pinot Noir from the Montagne contributes structure and Meunier adds generosity and fruit.

Winemaking

Traditional method with secondary fermentation in bottle. The key distinction is the extended lees ageing: six years in Magnum. The liqueur d'expédition is uniquely solera-based, adding yet another layer of complexity. Dosage is approximately 6.9 g/l, placing it firmly in Brut territory while retaining a subtle roundness.

Tasting Notes

Fresh, bracing acidity with extremely fine and impressively vigorous bubbles. The nose reveals apples, elderflower and hedgerow fruits, with an underlying richness from the extended ageing. On the palate there is rare fullness and maturity: the grace and power of Pinot Noir, the generosity of Meunier, and the precision of Chardonnay all in balance. The finish is long and satisfying, with a creamy, biscuity depth.

The Producer

Gimonnet-Gonet is the union of two Champagne dynasties. Anne Gonet and Philippe Gimonnet, each born into families who have made Champagne for generations, released their first bottles together in 1986. Their son Charles joined in 2012 and daughter Anne-Sophie in 2020, continuing what has become a generational pursuit of great Champagne.

What makes this house distinctive is its deep roots in the Côte des Blancs. Nearly all of their 13.5 hectares lie in the Grand Cru villages of Chouilly, Cramant, Oiry, Oger and Le Mesnil-sur-Oger. This is Chardonnay country, and the vast majority of their range reflects that identity: precise, mineral-driven blanc de blancs.

Which is exactly why this wine exists. They call it "L'Étonnant" (the surprising one), and the name is not accidental. In a portfolio built almost entirely on white grapes, this is their sole Blanc de Noirs, a deliberate departure sourced from Pinot Noir and Meunier parcels in the Vallée de la Marne. It is their way of showing that a house defined by Chardonnay finesse can also speak the language of Pinot with equal conviction.

Viticulture

The fruit comes from carefully selected Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier parcels in the Vallée de la Marne, where warmer exposures and mixed chalk-clay soils lend both structure and aromatic depth. These are established vineyards with the maturity to produce grapes of concentration and complexity, quite different in character from the family's Côte des Blancs holdings.

Winemaking

A Blanc de Noirs by definition: a white Champagne made exclusively from black-skinned grapes. The pressing must be gentle and precise to extract clean juice without colour from the skins. The base wine undergoes traditional method secondary fermentation in bottle, followed by extended lees ageing that builds the creamy texture and biscuity depth. The blend includes a high volume of Grand Cru-level fruit, giving this wine a pedigree that outclasses many big-name Champagnes at higher price points.

Tasting Notes

Of rare elegance, this Blanc de Noirs brings together finesse, structure and depth in a remarkably precise balance. The nose is rich and ripe, with red berry fruit, stone fruit and a subtle breadth that comes from the Pinot Noir dominance. On the palate there is body and generosity, balanced by fresh, zesty acidity and chalky minerality. The mousse is fine and persistent. It is both delicious and surprising: proof that Gimonnet-Gonet's mastery extends well beyond their Chardonnay heartland.

The Producer

Drappier is Champagne's quiet rebel. Based in Urville in the Côte des Bar, the southernmost part of Champagne, the family has been making wine here since 1808. They are a Pinot Noir house at heart: 70% of their vineyards are planted to it, and their most famous wines celebrate its depth and richness. The estate has been certified organic since 2023, working with minimal sulfur and a philosophy that the best intervention is none at all.

So when Drappier makes a Blanc de Blancs, it is not their default, it is a deliberate choice. They source Chardonnay from their chalk soils and add a touch of Blanc Vrai (the local name for Pinot Blanc), which brings a softness and ripeness that differentiates this from the leaner, more austere Blanc de Blancs of the Côte des Blancs. It is opulent where others are angular, generous where others are strict.

Viticulture

From chalk soils in the Côte des Bar. The southern location means slightly warmer conditions than the Côte des Blancs, allowing both Chardonnay and Pinot Blanc to reach full ripeness while retaining freshness from the chalky subsoil. Organic farming since 2023, with vineyard work that prioritises soil health.

Winemaking

Low-intervention vinification with minimal sulfur dosage during bottling. Traditional method with secondary fermentation in bottle and extended lees ageing. The gentle approach preserves the purity and fruit character of the grapes, resulting in a Blanc de Blancs that is more about pleasure than austerity.

Tasting Notes

Limpid colour with a fine, delicate mousse. The nose is classic and gourmet: citrus, white flowers and hints of pastry. On the palate it is opulent and intense, emphasising rich fruit (ripe apple, lemon juice, apricot) as much as minerality. There is fine depth of flavour and a fragrant, refreshing finish. Well balanced between richness and freshness.

Billecart-Salmon, Nicolas François Billecart, Brut Magnum, Champagne 2008
ProducerBillecart-Salmon (family-owned)

The Producer

Billecart-Salmon has been family-owned since 1818, making it one of the last major independent Champagne houses. Their philosophy has always been one of restraint and time. While much of the industry races to release wines young, Billecart pushes in the opposite direction: their non-vintage Brut guarantees at least three years on the lees, and their prestige cuvées wait far longer.

The Nicolas François Billecart, named after the house's founder, is their statement wine. The 2008 is perhaps the very last of the great 2008 Champagnes to reach the market, released 15 years after harvest. It spent over 150 months on its lees before being disgorged in the third quarter of 2022 with a dosage of just 2.9 g/l, barely perceptible. This is Champagne as an exercise in time and patience: the profound precision and molecular detail that define Billecart are transported to a higher level of fidelity by the legendary 2008 vintage.

Viticulture

Grand Cru fruit from the finest classified villages: Chardonnay from Le Mesnil-sur-Oger, Chouilly and Cramant in the Côte des Blancs, Pinot Noir from Aÿ, Mareuil and Verzenay in the Montagne de Reims. These are among the most revered terroirs in Champagne, and the 60/40 Pinot-to-Chardonnay ratio gives the wine both structure and elegance.

Winemaking

Partial vinification in traditional old oak casks (17%), with the remaining 83% in tanks. This oak element underpins the generous character without ever dominating. Over 12 years on the lees in Billecart's cellars, then disgorged with minimal dosage. The Magnum format allows for an even more graceful evolution during this extended ageing.

Tasting Notes

Rich straw hue. The nose is layered and long: truffle, apricot, croissant, floral orange blossoms and marmalade. A saline chalk core erupts from its depths, framing pure lemon fruit and strawberry hull. It is emphatically, sublimely primary, as if time has only concentrated rather than evolved its character. With air, Pinot steps forward in gloriously pure red cherry fruits. A brilliant display of fine chalk minerality at its most profound.

Vintage 2008

The 2008 growing season started inauspiciously: spring frosts were followed by hail, and July was cold and rainy. However, a frigid winter had rested the vines well, and conditions improved dramatically from August onward. The summer was dry with constant temperatures and normal rainfall, an absence of extremes that allowed slow, steady grape maturation. By September, hand-picking began on the 15th in ideal sunny conditions with healthy grapes showing a perfect sugar-acidity balance. The vintage is often compared to 1988 for its classical elegance and longevity. Krug nicknamed it "Classic Beauty." Bollinger's cellarmaster Gilles Descôtes later called it a "miracle year" given how badly it began. This bottling is its ultimate late expression.

The Producer

The story of La Grande Dame is the story of Madame Clicquot herself. Widowed at 27 in 1805, Barbe-Nicole Clicquot-Ponsardin took over her husband's wine business and became one of the greatest entrepreneurs of any age. Her innovations shaped Champagne as we know it: she produced the first known vintage Champagne, invented the riddling table (remuage), and in 1818, created the first blended rosé Champagne.

That first rosé came from a particular 1.3-hectare plot in Bouzy called Clos Colin, which she identified and nurtured specifically to provide an ideal red wine for her assemblage. Two centuries later, La Grande Dame Rosé continues to honour that tradition, using still red Pinot Noir blended into white base wine, the very technique Madame Clicquot pioneered when the idea of blending red and white was still controversial.

La Grande Dame is the house's prestige cuvée, named after the title Madame Clicquot's peers gave her. The rosé uses fruit exclusively from Grand Cru vineyards and is aged for at least seven years before release.

Viticulture

Pinot Noir dominates the blend, sourced from Grand Cru vineyards in the Montagne de Reims where it provides structure and depth. Chardonnay from the Côte des Blancs adds finesse and lift. The still red wine component comes from Pinot Noir vineyards in the tradition established by Madame Clicquot's original Bouzy selection.

Winemaking

Rosé d'assemblage: still red wine from Pinot Noir is blended with the white base wine before secondary fermentation in bottle. This is the method Madame Clicquot invented in 1818. Extended lees ageing of at least seven years builds the creamy texture, toasty depth and complexity expected at prestige cuvée level. Meticulous selection and blending by the cellar master.

Tasting Notes

Pale salmon-copper colour with fine, persistent bubbles. The nose offers red fruit (strawberry, raspberry, redcurrant), floral notes of rose, and hints of spice and brioche developed during the extended ageing. On the palate it is structured and vinous, silky yet powerful, with a creamy mousse, vibrant acidity and a long, elegant finish. Minerally at its core, fresh despite its richness.

Vintage 2015

The summer of 2015 was notably arid in Champagne, with minimal rainfall from late May to mid-August, creating hydric stress particularly challenging for Pinot Noir. High temperatures pushed ABVs above 13% in the best sites, a rare occurrence in this region. Crucially, the right amount of rain arrived in mid-to-late August, giving stressed vines exactly what they needed at the last moment. Cool nights prevented acidity levels from collapsing. Harvest started on 7 September under bright conditions, but picking was complicated by heterogeneous ripening caused by the drought, and heavy showers battered the vineyards within the first week. Bollinger described wrestling to contain the "opulence" of the year. The result: exceptional red-skinned grapes with remarkable maturity, powerful fruit and full body.

Taittinger 'Comtes de Champagne Rosé', Brut, Champagne 2007

The Producer

The name "Comtes de Champagne" reaches back to the medieval Counts of Champagne, who ruled the region centuries before anyone thought to put bubbles in a bottle. When the Taittinger family acquired the ancient residence of these Counts in Reims in 1932, they inherited something far more valuable than a building: the chalk cellars of the former Abbey of Saint-Nicaise, carved as quarries during the Gallo-Roman era, where their finest wines still age today.

The Comtes de Champagne is Taittinger's ultimate expression. The Blanc de Blancs version (100% Chardonnay) is already legendary. The Rosé is rarer still: produced only in exceptional years, it is one of the most sought-after rosé Champagnes in the world. While the house was founded in 1734, it is the combination of these ancient cellars, almost 300 hectares of vineyard (one of the largest estates in Champagne), and an unwavering insistence on quality that sets Taittinger apart.

Viticulture

Exclusively Grand Cru fruit. The Pinot Noir comes from the finest plots in the Montagne de Reims, while Chardonnay is sourced from the Côte des Blancs. The rosé colour is achieved through the addition of still Bouzy Rouge, red wine from Pinot Noir grown in the Grand Cru village of Bouzy.

Winemaking

The still red wine from Bouzy is blended with the white base before secondary fermentation. The wine then ages on its lees in Taittinger's Gallo-Roman chalk cellars for over 12 years. At nearly 15 years of age before release, this 2007 has had time to develop extraordinary complexity and integration. The chalk cellars maintain a constant cool temperature that allows this slow, graceful evolution.

Tasting Notes

Delicate salmon-pink hue with copper reflections and fine, persistent bubbles. The nose is complex and evolved: wild strawberry, blood orange, dried rose petals, praline and toasted brioche. On the palate it is vinous and structured, with creamy texture, bright acidity, and layers of red fruit, spice and mineral complexity that unfold over several minutes. The finish is exceptionally long and refined, a testament to both the vintage and the extraordinary time spent in those ancient cellars.

Vintage 2007

Challenging weather during spring and summer had many Champagne producers worried: conditions were warm and humid with rain through much of the growing season, and hail badly hit the Aube (Côte des Bar). However, a brilliant August and warm, dry September made for an outstanding but small crop of Pinot Noir, described as the best since 1952. Chardonnays were bigger and more foursquare, not in the same league as the Pinots that year. This makes the 2007 a particularly strong vintage for Pinot Noir-dominant cuvées, which is precisely what the Comtes de Champagne Rosé is.

The Producer

Palmer & Co was founded in 1947 by seven grower families in Avize who decided, in the shadow of the Second World War, that pooling their Grand Cru and Premier Cru vineyards would produce better wine than going it alone. They moved to Reims in 1959, acquired cellars, and built what is today a cooperative of over 200 members farming across 40 crus in the Montagne de Reims and Côte des Blancs.

What separates Palmer from other cooperatives is the seriousness of the winemaking. Under chef de cave Xavier Berdin, the house ages its wines far longer than the appellation demands and uses old Burgundian oak casks and perpetual reserves to build complexity. The Rosé Solera is perhaps the most distinctive wine in their range: a rosé Champagne whose colour and depth come not from simple blending or saignée, but from a solera of Pinot Noir that has been running continuously for over forty years. It is a technique borrowed from Jerez, applied to Champagne, producing something with a memory that stretches back decades.

Viticulture

The fruit is sourced from Premier Cru and Grand Cru vineyards across the Montagne de Reims, where the chalk soils and northern exposures give Pinot Noir its characteristic structure and depth. Chardonnay comes from classified parcels in the Côte des Blancs, contributing freshness and precision to the blend.

Winemaking

Traditional method with secondary fermentation in bottle. The defining element here is the solera: 8% of the final blend comes from a perpetual reserve of Pinot Noir started over forty years ago and maintained in stainless steel. Each year, a portion is drawn off for blending and replenished with fresh wine, meaning the solera carries a thread of every vintage since its inception. The remainder of the blend undergoes partial fermentation in 15-year-old Burgundian oak barrels for eight to ten months before assemblage. Reserve wines make up 30–35% of the blend. The wine is aged on the lees for three years in standard bottles and five years in Magnum. Dosage is approximately 8 g/l.

Tasting Notes

A vibrant, pale rose-gold in the glass with a fine, persistent bead. The nose is intense and layered: wild strawberry leads, followed by red and black currants, delicate vanilla, cinnamon spice, and a pastry-like richness from the extended lees ageing. On the palate it is fresh yet full-bodied, with a soft creaminess and fine tannins from the solera Pinot Noir. Red fruit persists through the mid-palate, joined by notes of lychee and a subtle ginger warmth on the finish. The mousse is refined and the length impressive. It is a rosé Champagne with genuine depth and memory.

The Producer

Coates & Seely was born from a consoling bottle of Champagne and a lost rugby match. In 2007, on the eve of the Rugby World Cup final, Nicholas Coates travelled to Bordeaux to support his old friend Christian Seely, who was living in a country of 60 million people all wanting England to lose. England did lose. Halfway through the second consoling bottle of Champagne, Coates said: "We live in Hampshire, where we have chalk slopes. Have you ever thought of making English sparkling wine?"

The idea stuck. Nick Coates, a retired City financier, and Christian Seely, the managing director of AXA Millésimes' wine division (responsible for Château Pichon-Longueville, Château Suduiraut and Quinta do Noval) brought together English ambition and world-class winemaking experience. They planted their Hampshire chalk slopes with Chardonnay, Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier, the same varieties, and geologically identical chalk, as Champagne.

They call their method "méthode Britannique" with a wink. Production is deliberately small-scale and artisanal (around 45,000 bottles a year), allowing them to make wines to the most exacting standards in their own unique way.

Viticulture

The vineyards sit on chalk soils in Hampshire that are geologically identical to those of the Champagne region. This chalk provides the mineral backbone, excellent drainage and high natural acidity that are essential for producing world-class sparkling wine. The cool English climate means a long growing season with gradual ripening.

Winemaking

Traditional method with secondary fermentation in bottle. The Brut Réserve is a blend of 65% Chardonnay and 35% Pinot Noir, incorporating reserves from previous years for depth and consistency. Lees-aged for 2.5 to 3 years, building the creamy texture and biscuity complexity that characterise the best traditional method wines.

Tasting Notes

Richness with notes of apples, elderflower and hedgerow fruits. The palate is lean and structured, with high acidity, sweet-and-sour sensations and a fine chalky minerality. Long, succulent and brisk on the finish. A wine that is unmistakably English in its freshness, yet entirely serious in its ambition.

The Producer

The same partnership as the Réserve: Nicholas Coates and Christian Seely, two old friends from business school whose shared passion for wine led them from a lost rugby match in Bordeaux to planting chalk slopes in Hampshire. Christian's day job running Pichon-Longueville, Suduiraut and Quinta do Noval means this small English estate benefits from a depth of winemaking knowledge normally reserved for the world's greatest châteaux.

While the Réserve showcases Chardonnay elegance, the Rosé flips the script entirely. It is built on Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier, the same black-skinned varieties that give body and fruit to the finest rosé Champagnes. Where the Réserve is all precision and chalk, the Rosé is warmth, generosity and red-fruit charm.

Viticulture

Same Hampshire chalk as the Réserve. The Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier grown here benefit from the cool English climate, which preserves acidity and freshness while allowing the fruit to develop aromatic complexity during the long growing season.

Winemaking

Traditional method. The rosé colour is achieved through brief skin contact with Pinot Noir before pressing, extracting just enough pigment and flavour without tannin. Extended lees ageing of over 2.5 years develops the creamy mousse and complexity. The blend of 65% Pinot Noir and 35% Pinot Meunier gives structure from the former and fruity generosity from the latter.

Tasting Notes

A joyful English sparkling rosé. The colour is a delicate pink, and the nose opens with big notes of red fruits: strawberry, raspberry, redcurrant. There is a slightly sweet fruitiness that is immediately appealing. On the palate it is fresh and elegant, with a fine mousse, crisp acidity and enough body to hold its own at the table. Charming and precise in equal measure.

Coates & Seely, La Perfide Rosé, Brut, Hampshire, England 2014

The Producer

The same Coates & Seely story: Nicholas Coates and Christian Seely, born from a consoling bottle of Champagne after England lost the 2007 Rugby World Cup final. But "La Perfide" is where the duo's quiet English humour comes fully into its own.

The name is a deliberate provocation. "La Perfide Albion" is how the French have referred to England for centuries: perfidious, treacherous, not to be trusted. Coates & Seely borrowed the insult and made it their prestige cuvée, a vintage wine produced only in exceptional years and intended to prove that English sparkling can rival the very best of Champagne. The joke landed rather well when the original La Perfide Blanc de Blancs 2009 won the inaugural UK Wine Awards supreme champion in 2017, beating nearly 300 English and Welsh competitors. Its sister wine, La Perfide Rosé, took best sparkling rosé in the same competition.

Where the standard Rosé NV is charming and accessible, La Perfide Rosé is something else entirely. It is vintage-dated, longer-aged, and made only when the fruit merits it. It represents the absolute pinnacle of what this small Hampshire estate can produce in pink.

Viticulture

The grapes come from Coates & Seely's own chalk slopes near Whitchurch in North Hampshire. The soils are geologically identical to those of the Champagne region: pure chalk providing excellent drainage, mineral complexity and the high natural acidity essential for serious sparkling wine. The 2014 growing season in England was exceptional — a long, warm spring and summer followed by a dry September that allowed an early harvest of physiologically ripe fruit in large quantities. Producers across England described it as a once-in-a-generation vintage.

Winemaking

Traditional method, or as Coates & Seely call it with characteristic understatement, "méthode Britannique." The rosé colour is achieved through brief skin contact with Pinot Noir before pressing, extracting pigment and aromatic complexity without tannin. As a prestige cuvée, La Perfide receives significantly extended lees ageing compared to the standard NV Rosé, building the autolytic complexity, creamy texture and biscuity depth that distinguish it. This is a deliberately small-production wine from a house that already makes only around 45,000 bottles a year across its entire range.

Tasting Notes

A deeper, more intense pink than the NV Rosé. The nose is rich and complex: sour cherry, red plum, truffle, and autolytic notes of brioche and biscuit. The palate has genuine depth and structure — red fruits are concentrated and layered, balanced by electrifying English acidity and a chalky minerality that speaks directly of the Hampshire chalk beneath the vines. The mousse is fine and persistent, the finish long and savoury. This is not a light summer rosé; it is a vintage wine of real ambition and complexity.

Vintage 2014

England's 2014 vintage was widely described as exceptional and record-breaking. A mild spring led to early budbreak, followed by a warm, dry summer that gave healthy canopies and abundant fruit set. September brought the dry warmth needed for final ripening, and harvest came earlier than usual with ripe, concentrated grapes in generous quantities. UK wine production hit record highs. Multiple producers, including those in Hampshire, described it as "the vintage of dreams" — a year of natural ripeness and balance that occurs perhaps once a decade in the English climate.

The Producer

Adegas Valmiñor was founded in 1997 by Carlos Gómez Davila in O Rosal, one of the three original subzones of DO Rías Baixas. The name "Davila" is the surname of the family and means "from the village" in Galician, which is why they chose it for this personal line of wines with limited production. The winemaker is Cristina Mantilla.

While most of the world associates Rías Baixas with monovarietal Albariño, this cuvée tells a different story. It is a blend of three indigenous grapes (Albariño, Loureiro and Treixadura), a nod to the traditional field blends that existed in O Rosal long before single-variety bottlings became fashionable. The result is a wine with more complexity and textural interest than a straight Albariño, reflecting the full diversity of what this Atlantic corner of Spain can offer.

Viticulture

The grapes are sourced from 20 to 40-year-old vines planted on sandy alluvial and metamorphic schist soils in the O Rosal subzone, the southernmost area of Rías Baixas where the Miño river meets the Atlantic Ocean. The mild Atlantic climate, free from dramatic temperature swings, provides ideal conditions for slow, even ripening of indigenous white varieties. All fruit is hand-harvested to ensure only healthy, optimally ripe clusters reach the winery.

Winemaking

Upon arrival at the winery, the grapes are destemmed and given a cold soak to extract primary aromatic compounds before fermentation begins. Fermentation takes place in stainless steel tanks at a controlled temperature of approximately 18 °C over roughly 20 days, preserving freshness and varietal expression. The wine then rests on its fine lees to build texture and complexity. No oak is used at any stage, keeping the focus on purity of fruit and mineral character.

Tasting Notes

Pale yellow in colour with greenish reflections. The nose is aromatic and intense, offering layers of citrus (grapefruit and lemon) alongside stone fruit such as white peach and nectarine, lifted by delicate floral hints and a distinctive saline mineral undertone. On the palate the wine is fresh and structured, with vibrant acidity driving flavours of ripe citrus and a subtle mineral salinity. The finish is medium-long with a pleasant, slightly bitter edge that invites another sip.

Vintage 2024

Harvest 2024 in Galicia was slightly lower in volume than 2023, but all signs point to a great year for quality. The summer was very warm and dry, meaning mildew was not an issue for once in this notoriously damp region (Rías Baixas averages 1,600mm of rainfall annually, nearly three times the Spanish average). Good weather throughout the growing season allowed for a slow, complete ripening. Notably, Rías Baixas experienced its third-largest harvest on record (behind only 2017 and 2021). Temperatures have been steadily rising over the past decade, with 2014 the last vintage where Albariño was picked in October; September is now standard, with some producers picking in late August.

The Producer

Mendes & Symington is a joint venture between two wine families with very different pedigrees that somehow make perfect sense together. Symington Family Estates has been making Port since 1882 and is responsible for Graham's, Dow's and Warre's, among others. Anselmo Mendes is widely considered the visionary of modern Vinho Verde, the winemaker who proved that this region could produce wines of seriousness and complexity, not just simple summer sippers.

Fermentation followed by ageing in French oak barrels, initially in new oak, then continuing in a combination of new and seasoned barrels. The blend is assembled to express the signature Apalta character of earthiness and power while remaining more immediately accessible than the Grand Vin.

Pétrus is, simply, the most famous Merlot in the world. It carries no classification (Pomerol has none) yet outprices every First Growth in the Médoc. There is no château building, no grand entrance, no corporate ownership. Just 11.4 hectares of vineyard, a modest winery, and one wine.

The vineyard sits at the highest point of the Pomerol plateau on a seam of blue clay that is approximately 40 million years old, laced with iron-rich deposits known locally as crasse de fer. Viticulture

The vineyard sits at the highest point of the Pomerol plateau, approximately 40 metres above sea level. Two layers of clay: the topsoil of dark clay is 60 to 80 centimetres thick, and below it lies the unique subsoil of very dense, deep, dark blue clay not found in any other vineyard. 100% Merlot. Hand-harvested with meticulous sorting.

Winemaking

Fermentation in concrete vats. Ageing in 50% new French oak barrels for approximately 18 to 20 months. The winemaking is one of precision and restraint, allowing the extraordinary terroir to express itself fully.

Tasting Notes

The wine opens with an extremely precise, controlled bouquet, layering ripe black cherries, truffle and crystalline red and black fruit. More mineral character emerges than in some recent vintages. On the palate it reveals beautiful mid-palate depth, sweet tannins, and a voluptuous yet weightless texture that needs to be tasted to be believed. A masterclass in balance, reconciling strength with finesse.

The Right Bank excelled particularly that year, with Pomerol widely considered its strongest appellation. The Wine Cellar Insider described the best 2015 Pomerols as "bottled decadence, hedonistic thrill rides of almost unequalled sensual pleasure," comparing them to the best 2009s. For Pétrus specifically, the 2015 was aged in 50% new oak, and tasters noted more mineral character than in some recent vintages, perhaps because there was less "puppy fat" obscuring the terroir expression.
