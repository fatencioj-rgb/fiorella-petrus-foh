## The Producer

The same Coates & Seely story: Nicholas Coates and Christian Seely, born from a consoling bottle of Champagne after England lost the 2007 Rugby World Cup final. But "La Perfide" is where the duo's quiet English humour comes fully into its own.

The name is a deliberate provocation. "La Perfide Albion" is how the French have referred to England for centuries: perfidious, treacherous, not to be trusted. Coates & Seely borrowed the insult and made it their prestige cuvée, a vintage wine produced only in exceptional years and intended to prove that English sparkling can rival the very best of Champagne. The joke landed rather well when the original La Perfide Blanc de Blancs 2009 won the inaugural UK Wine Awards supreme champion in 2017, beating nearly 300 English and Welsh competitors. Its sister wine, La Perfide Rosé, took best sparkling rosé in the same competition.

Where the standard Rosé NV is charming and accessible, La Perfide Rosé is something else entirely. It is vintage-dated, longer-aged, and made only when the fruit merits it. It represents the absolute pinnacle of what this small Hampshire estate can produce in pink.

## Viticulture

The grapes come from Coates & Seely's own chalk slopes near Whitchurch in North Hampshire. The soils are geologically identical to those of the Champagne region: pure chalk providing excellent drainage, mineral complexity and the high natural acidity essential for serious sparkling wine. The 2014 growing season in England was exceptional — a long, warm spring and summer followed by a dry September that allowed an early harvest of physiologically ripe fruit in large quantities. Producers across England described it as a once-in-a-generation vintage.

## Winemaking

Traditional method, or as Coates & Seely call it with characteristic understatement, "méthode Britannique." The rosé colour is achieved through brief skin contact with Pinot Noir before pressing, extracting pigment and aromatic complexity without tannin. As a prestige cuvée, La Perfide receives significantly extended lees ageing compared to the standard NV Rosé, building the autolytic complexity, creamy texture and biscuity depth that distinguish it. This is a deliberately small-production wine from a house that already makes only around 45,000 bottles a year across its entire range.

## Tasting Notes

A deeper, more intense pink than the NV Rosé. The nose is rich and complex: sour cherry, red plum, truffle, and autolytic notes of brioche and biscuit. The palate has genuine depth and structure — red fruits are concentrated and layered, balanced by electrifying English acidity and a chalky minerality that speaks directly of the Hampshire chalk beneath the vines. The mousse is fine and persistent, the finish long and savoury. This is not a light summer rosé; it is a vintage wine of real ambition and complexity.

## Vintage 2014

England's 2014 vintage was widely described as exceptional and record-breaking. A mild spring led to early budbreak, followed by a warm, dry summer that gave healthy canopies and abundant fruit set. September brought the dry warmth needed for final ripening, and harvest came earlier than usual with ripe, concentrated grapes in generous quantities. UK wine production hit record highs. Multiple producers, including those in Hampshire, described it as "the vintage of dreams" — a year of natural ripeness and balance that occurs perhaps once a decade in the English climate.
