## The Producer

Pessac-Léognan is the appellation that Bordeaux insiders drink when they want serious wine without the price tag of the Médoc's classified growths. Château La Garde, with its 54 hectares of vineyard on classic Graves terroir, makes benchmark wines from this area: deep, structured and well-balanced clarets that quietly over-deliver.

The estate's terroir is typical of the best Pessac-Léognan: deep gravel beds over clay and limestone that provide excellent drainage and heat retention. The blend for this vintage leans on Cabernet Sauvignon (52%) for structure and ageability, supported by Merlot (45%) for roundness and a touch of Petit Verdot (3%) for colour and spice. With nearly a decade of bottle age, the 2016 is now in a beautiful drinking window, showing the kind of integration and complexity that reward patience.

## Viticulture

A 54-hectare estate on deep Graves gravel soils over clay and limestone. The 2016 growing season was characterised by a very wet first half followed by a very dry finish, which concentrated the fruit beautifully. The contrast between the two halves of the season is what gives the vintage its distinctive character of ripe fruit married with freshness.

## Winemaking

Traditional Bordelais vinification with temperature-controlled fermentation in stainless steel and concrete vats. Ageing in French oak barrels for approximately 14 to 16 months. The final blend is assembled to create the most complete expression of the vintage.

## Tasting Notes

Deep colour with an appealingly scented dark fruit bouquet. Aromas of cassis, dark plum and tobacco on the nose. On the palate there is good fruit density, depth and structure with refined tannins and a lingering finish. A classy, well-balanced claret.

## Vintage 2016

The first half of the 2016 growing season in Bordeaux was one of the wettest, cooler periods in recent history. By mid-June, according to several growers, the vintage looked like "a disaster in the making." Even vineyards with good drainage were simply too wet, with 750mm of rainfall recorded between January and 23 June. Then everything changed. From late June onward, many areas saw only a tenth of the 30-year average rainfall: Jancis Robinson reported parts of Bordeaux receiving just 14mm versus a normal 140mm over 12 weeks, the driest stretch in 25 years. The wet spring proved to be a hidden blessing: it gave vines the water reserves to survive this exceptional drought. Old vines with deep roots on gravel soils with good water retention relished the conditions. Cabernet Sauvignon excelled. The red wines that emerged are structured and firm, with deep colour and concentrated tannins. For Pessac-Léognan reds specifically, 2016 produced "future legendary" wines.
