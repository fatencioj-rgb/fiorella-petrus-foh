## The Producer

Château Beychevelle is a Fourth Growth (Quatrième Grand Cru Classé) of the legendary 1855 Classification, located in Saint-Julien. Its name comes from the legend of the Duc d'Épernon, Admiral of France, whose passing ships would "lower their sails" (baisser les voiles, becoming "Beychevelle" in Gascon dialect) as a mark of respect. The château's label still features its iconic griffin-prowed ship.

Les Brulières de Beychevelle is a separate wine, distinct from both the Grand Vin and the second wine (Amiral de Beychevelle). It comes from the estate's Haut-Médoc vineyards rather than their Saint-Julien plots, giving it a different appellation but the same meticulous attention. It is vinified at a separate winery but produced with the same level of care, expertise and ambition as the classified wines. Think of it as Beychevelle's philosophy applied to a more accessible terroir.

## Viticulture

From Château Beychevelle's Haut-Médoc vineyards. Classic Left Bank soils of gravel and clay providing excellent drainage and heat retention. The overall estate is planted to 52% Cabernet Sauvignon, 40% Merlot, 5% Cabernet Franc and 3% Petit Verdot.

## Winemaking

Traditional Bordelais vinification with temperature-controlled fermentation and ageing in oak barrels. The same winemaking team and quality standards as the classified wines, applied with the same precision and rigour.

## Tasting Notes

A delightful blend of black fruits, blackcurrant, blackberries, violet, cedarwood, licorice, and spices. Rich yet not overwhelming density, with dark fruit notes and a touch of chocolate, all wrapped in a smooth, silky texture. Refined tannins and a long, elegant finish.

## Vintage 2020

2020 in Bordeaux followed a now-familiar pattern: wet spring, dry hot summer, warm harvest. A mild, wet spring brought mildew problems that reduced yields. Then from 18 June to 11 August came fifty-four consecutive days of drought, almost unheard of. Temperatures in the low to mid-30s during September further concentrated berries. Jancis Robinson called it "a really good but variable year" and noted the pattern is similar to 2016, 2018 and 2019. For the Haut-Médoc (where Beychevelle sits), estates with older vines and water-retentive soils made the best wines. Despite the heat, 2020 is notably lower in alcohol than 2018.
