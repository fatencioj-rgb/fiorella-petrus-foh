## The Producer

Sancerre rouge is one of wine's great secrets. While the world drinks Sancerre white without a second thought, very few people realise that this appellation also produces some of France's most elegant Pinot Noir outside Burgundy. Pierre Morin is one of the people making that case.

Pierre studied in Burgundy and worked at Domaine Dujac under Jeremy Seysses, an experience that shaped his entire approach to Pinot Noir. He returned to the family estate in Bué, three kilometres from Sancerre, with the conviction that great wine can only come from low yields, hand-harvesting and meticulous sorting. He prefers to work naturally in both vineyard and cellar, avoiding herbicides and using indigenous yeasts for fermentation.

The family's vines are planted on a steep hard-calcaire (Kimmeridgian limestone) amphitheatre surrounding Bué, the same type of limestone that defines Chablis. It gives the reds a freshness and mineral precision that distinguish them from Burgundy Pinot Noir, making them lighter, crunchier and immediately charming.

## Viticulture

Steep slopes of Kimmeridgian limestone surrounding the commune of Bué. Low yields from carefully tended vines. No herbicides. Hand-harvested with meticulous sorting in the vineyard.

## Winemaking

Fermentation uses natural yeasts. The wine is partly aged in wood (barrels), which adds structure and subtle complexity while preserving the fresh, fruit-driven character. The approach is focused on balance between red fruit and acidity, with polished texture from the partial wood ageing.

## Tasting Notes

Bright and expressive on the nose, showing ripe cherry and fresh strawberry aromas layered with subtle spice and cedar nuances. On the palate, the wine is juicy and vibrant, driven by a core of crunchy red fruit and refreshing acidity. The texture is smooth and soft, with fine tannins and a succulent, polished finish.

## Vintage 2023

Sancerre's 2023 growing season in the Loire Valley benefited from the same general pattern seen across France's northern regions: a wet spring that caused concern, followed by a warm, dry summer and extended autumn that allowed full maturity. The Kimmeridgian limestone soils around Bué drain well, which helped mitigate the wet start. For Pinot Noir in Sancerre specifically, the warmth of recent vintages has been transformative: the grape achieves a ripeness and depth unimaginable two decades ago, when red Sancerre was considered a minor curiosity. Pierre Morin's low-yielding, hand-harvested approach further concentrated the fruit.
